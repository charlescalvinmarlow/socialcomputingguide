How to evaluate an image on Instagram

A Social Computing Guide
by Charles Marlow

Introduction

“We all compete for status, whether we like it or not. . . . We can at least better explain the rules to make it a fairer fight." This quote from David Marx (2022) highlights an aspect of human society and culture that while predating social media, is also inextricably linked to our use of it. Engagement with social media is something often done from a young age, in a very casual manner and without critical engagement, so we subconsciously learn through the use of the system and once we have learnt how the system works we "share content based on the evaluation [we] expect from other users." (Trillò et al, 2023). As we learn the conventions of social media and in turn participate in them, it seems worthwhile to stop and ask, what exactly are the rules here? How can I define the values I deem worthwhile in social media? I don't seek to have those answers for you, or suggest that there is one true set of values, they will shift based on a variety of factors, but I do believe there are tools, frameworks and questions that if you take the time to utilise, can help you understand the sometimes vague feelings you may have when using Instagram.

Instagram

What began as a way to snap a photo and share it immediately, Instagram users have shifted to engaging in what Leaver et al (2020) describe as a "particular effort in the planning for photosharing … The most common strategies can be summed up as a good quality of photography, particularity and unconventionality of scenarios, creativity of the visual composition and images able to convey emotions and feeling." Despite appearances, Instagram is not simply a place where you can share an image thoughtlessly and continue with your day. Because every photo comes with the expectation of engagement, Trillò et all (2023) suggest "Each photo makes a public statement about what is worthwhile and, in turn, reaffirms broad socio-cultural frameworks through which objects are assigned value." These socio-cultural frameworks are the ones we learn as we engage with the internet, the internet that is an exceptional tool at teaching how it should be used within its own social context. We sign up for Instagram, see what our friends, celebrities, brands and others are doing, see the reaction (positive and negative) they get, and moderate our own usage based on the information we have gathered (Tonkinwise and Titmarsh, 2025). This unconscious learning is part of what this guide will seek to engage with, to help you see behind the "constant process of human and machinic evaluation" (Trillò et al, 2023) that occurs while you browse images and occasionally create and post your own. It is also important to note the increasing control of Algorithms, and the corporate control of Meta on what you see, and their, admittedly secretive and vague, ability to control what is presented before, and what values are contained within. If you are able to better cognize the values you hold and are engaging with the images with, and better understand the values content you encounter presents and how you feel about those values, you will be able to make using Instagram feel like a more worthwhile experience, making it match your values, and not letting it have as much control on what those values are.

Guide

In this guide we will dive into literature on different types of images and their value, in and out of social media, in order to give you the tools to critically engage with the hundreds, or possibly thousands of images you encounter on social media day to day.

We have organised the guide into sections of images that people have noted as being something they either often engage with or have thoughts about when they come across them on Instagram.

Perhaps what Instagram is most famous for, portraits are the images we share of ourselves, or our friends and family to express who we are, in the way that we want other people to see us. Portraits have existed as a way to signal the values of the person depicted for a long time, and the social rules around it have grown alongside that long history. As Trillò et al (2023) put it, "Over time, commonly adopted self-presentation strategies coalesce into conventions defining what is photographable or, more recently, Instagrammable." Trillò et al also suggest that "In the context of social media, the opportunity to express one’s values is bound up with the obligation to be evaluated by others and enrolled in complex systems of algorithmic valuation".

In their attempt to research values that are placed on portraits within social media, Trillò et al conclude that the most universal sets of values are communicative ones, does the image communicate itself as good social media? The assumption here is when evaluating a posted image, we emphasise the fact that someone *shared* this image, they decided it should be public and we critique that decision based on our personal set of values. For example, the most frequently encountered value in Trillò et al's research was *authenticity*. Authenticity is not an easy concept to define, and yet I think we all are capable of evaluating an image within moments by our own criteria of what is authentic. Research has suggested some of the factors we use to evaluate authenticity include poses, facial gestures, editing, and assumptions about the production, was it truly candid or meticulously staged and distinct from the posters reality. (Trillò et al, 2023)

Questions

Below is a list of some questions you are likely already answering. When you engage with these photos next time consider the questions more deeply, and then consider why these are things you consider in the first place.

  • What is included in the image—and what is left out?
  • Is this moment posed or candid? How can I tell?
  • What is the likely intention behind posting this image? (Affirmation? Status? Connection? Humor? Validation?)
  • What kind of identity is being performed or presented here?
  • Who benefits from the aesthetics or message in this image?

Fashion while an important vessel of self expression, is well known to utilise social media as a marketing arena. Pham (2022) discusses how our values and ethics within fashion, are often mobilised by corporations in what Pham describes as regulative work. Pham describes an accusation of IP theft, taken to social media for extralegal justice, that she argues is framed through a lens of values that perpetuate racialised and gendered terms. "Ethical fashion’s social contract dictates that consumers share in the responsibility of protecting the purity of the (Western) fashion- design market from the flood of illegitimate/fake Asian products threatening to encroach on and corrupt it." (Pham, 2022). It is the nature of social media, that nuances such as this are easily passed over as you share the image and move on to the next one, but the reality is, these complexities exist in almost every image you come across.

Below is a list of some questions you are likely already answering. When you engage with these photos next time consider the questions more deeply, and then consider why these are things you consider in the first place.

  • What am I being sold, explicitly or implicitly (a product, a lifestyle, a mood, a fantasy)?
  • Who is represented, and who is missing?
  • Am I inspired, envious, curious, inferior, indifferent?
  • Am I engaging with this content because I value it, or because the algorithm pushes it?
  • Does the fashion shown reflect current social values—or distract from them?

Art is always a difficult area to pin specific values on to, it is a discussion that has been happening since the term art has first existed. In his book *The Gift* (1999), Lewis Hyde argues that the value of Art, which is not one of practical capitalist production, is a gift that arrives to and passes through the artist and out into the world through their work. Thinking about it in a different way, Kidd (2018) raises the question, what is the distinction between art media and non-art media? Kidd mentions various way to make the distinction but they all have weaknesses and strengths, and in the end, Kidd posits that all human creative action is art, and we just call most of it media instead. These questions that Hyde and Kidd ponder in their writing is undoubtedly something you have done with social media too, some images on Instagram you would consider art, but the majority you likely would not? It is an interesting exercise to try and find and consider the boundaries you have put on the notions of media and art.

Conclusion

I think we have reached a point in time where often people use social media but feel a sense on unease about it. Trying to pin down exactly what your feelings are that are behind that uneasiness is not a simple matter, so this guide was intended as a way to engage with that feeling through the content you encounter as you use Instagram. When I spoke to people, they had vague senses of the values they were judging the images that crossed their screen by, but I think adding a level of structure and critique to that is of use.

For part A my research was mainly scholarly research, looking into systems of values for images, reading research about Instagram and the culture of use that has come with it. I also spoke to some friends and family about their use of the app and what kinds of images they engaged with, how they made them feel, and if they ever critically engaged with the content placed before them.

This guide's core idea is to reveal the cultural and individual value structures that are lurking in the dark behind every Instagram post to the viewer. To recognise how their value systems come into play when engaging with the images they encounter on social media every day, and to attempt to give them some understanding on the complexities that they might be feeling but not fully understanding.
